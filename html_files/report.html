Pipeline narrative what actually happened, in order

1

Parse 27 CIFs from the Apheris dataset

For each of the 10 train + 17 val PDE10A co-crystals, parsed the mmCIF (via gemmi) to extract per-chain protein sequence, the bound ligand as SMILES (via rdkit), and a binding-site residue mask — every polymer residue with at least one heavy atom within 5 Å of the ligand. Computed an MD5 hash of the SEQRES to group constructs.

2

Verify the MSA situation

Checked every _msa.a3m file Apheris shipped. Hit the headline finding immediately: all 27 a3ms are bit-identical (md5 8569ccaf...). The 28th file (5shr_alt1_msa.a3m) is an alternate construct's MSA. Apheris uses one canonical PDE10A catalytic-domain MSA across every entry. This collapsed augmentation MSA cost from "rate-limiting" to "free copy".

3

Embed proteins with ESM-2

Used the pretrained facebook/esm2_t12_35M_UR50D model to embed each complex's protein sequence (whole protein + pocket-only). Mean-pooled token embeddings. Built a 27×27 cosine similarity matrix on the pocket-only embedding — that's where the conservation signal sits, since all 27 share the PDE10A fold and whole-protein embeddings would look near-identical.

4

Embed ligands with RDKit Morgan FPs

Extracted the primary (largest non-solvent) ligand per CIF, computed Morgan-r2-2048 fingerprints, built the Tanimoto similarity matrix, and Butina-clustered at cutoff 0.65 (the chemotype-scaffold regime for fragment-sized ligands).

5

Joint similarity + UMAP projection

Combined the pocket-ESM cosine and ligand-Morgan-Tanimoto matrices into a joint score (mean). UMAP-projected the pocket-ESM embeddings to 2D. This is the picture in similarity_map.png below.

6

RCSB search — find more real PDE10A complexes

Hit the RCSB Search API with full-text "Q9Y233" → 359 PDB entries, all human PDE10A. Hit the GraphQL data API in chunks to pull metadata (SEQRES, ligand SMILES, resolution, deposit date) for every one. Cached everything under ~/biohackathon-work/cache/.

7

Filter to viable candidates

Dropped: (a) entries in our 27 (e.g. 5SHR was a hit), (b) entries with no bound small-molecule ligand, (c) entries whose SEQRES doesn't contain the canonical Apheris MSA query (different protein construct boundaries — these would need a fresh MSA). 271 of 359 candidates survived.

8

Score & chemotype-balanced pick

For each of the 271 candidates: computed max Tanimoto vs our train ligands and vs our val ligands; recorded the nearest neighbour in each set. Butina-clustered the candidate ligands too. Then picked 25 entries with two rules:

  • 3 picks per uncovered val ligand cluster (6 clusters × 3 = 18)
  • 7 cluster-diversity fillers from candidate clusters not yet sampled
9

Assemble augmentation_set/ on the VM

For each of the 25 picks: downloaded the CIF from RCSB, copied the canonical 5sdy_msa.a3m to <pdb_id>_msa.a3m, verified the CIF parses and has a ligand. Result: 25 paired CIF+a3m files (104 MB) at ~/biohackathon-work/similarity/augmentation_set/.

10

Propose the rebalanced split

Built split_proposal.json with three views: original (10/17, kept as reference), rebalanced (44 train / 8 eval — 10 originals + 9 reassigned from val + 25 augmentations; 8 held-out chosen one-per-ligand-cluster + 1 extra-far), and leave-one-ligand-cluster-out (7 stricter evaluation slices, one per val ligand cluster).

What we learn from the figure

  • Protein-side coverage looks uniform. All 27 pocket embeddings sit close together. Pocket geometry is not a useful discriminator within our 27 — we need the ligand side to separate them, which is exactly what the colours show.
  • Train clusters (squares) and val clusters (circles) sit in different colour groups. Train ligand clusters {0, 3, 4, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14}; val clusters {0, 1, 2, 5, 6, 7, 8}. Six of seven val clusters have no train representative.
  • This is the "domain adaptation" framing in one picture. The model is being asked to predict pose on a ligand chemotype it has not seen during fine-tuning — the published split is already a generalisation test.

How the augmentation is generated — and why 25, not 271 important — read this

Clarification: all 25 augmentation picks are REAL PDB entries

Each pick is a real X-ray crystal structure of human PDE10A bound to a ligand, deposited to the RCSB Protein Data Bank by researchers (mostly the XChem fragment screen at Diamond, with some lead-opt co-crystals from earlier campaigns). Nothing in the augmentation set is synthetic, simulated, generated, or hallucinated. The picks include entries like 5SH5, 5SK4, 4MSA, 4LM0, 4MRZ — all real crystal structures.

The word "augmentation" here is a data-curation term: we are augmenting the 10-entry training pool Apheris shipped by adding more real co-crystals from the public domain. There is no artificial-data injection at this stage.

The "synthetic structures via docking/diffusion" route — generating artificial poses with tools like AutoDock Vina or a diffusion model — is a Tier 4 fallback in our plan that we have NOT triggered and have no plans to trigger unless the first FT run shows specific chemotype gaps that real PDB data cannot fill.

How a pick becomes a usable training entry

A

Download the real CIF from RCSB

https://files.rcsb.org/download/<pdb_id>.cif — the official crystallographic file. Cached locally on the VM.

B

Reuse the canonical PDE10A MSA

Copy 5sdy_msa.a3m to <pdb_id>_msa.a3m. This works because (a) all 359 RCSB Q9Y233 entries share the UniProt protein sequence and (b) all 27 Apheris-supplied a3ms are bit-identical, so the same MSA applies. We do not generate any new alignment.

C

Sanity-check the CIF

Parse it with gemmi; confirm it has at least one protein chain and at least one bound non-solvent ligand. If either is missing, drop the pick. (None of our 25 were dropped at this stage.)

D

Drop into augmentation_set/ with the Apheris naming

The Hub UI expects pairs like <id>.cif + <id>_msa.a3m living together. We mirror that.

Why 25 of 271, not all 271

Why we picked 25 (current choice)

  • Hyperparameter safety. Apheris's reference recipe (learning_rate=0.0003, ema_decay=0.99) was tuned for N=10. Going from 10 → 44 (4.4×) keeps us within a safe regime; we expect the recipe to converge in the 10 h budget.
  • Chemotype diversity, not redundancy. 18 of 25 picks cover the 6 val-only ligand clusters (3 per cluster); 7 add candidate-cluster diversity. Over-sampling one scaffold wouldn't help.
  • Apheris reference proves the regime works. Their published run used 10. We're already at 44.
  • Wall-clock budget. 25 took ~10 min to assemble; left time for splits + brief + report.

Why we didn't take all 271

  • Hyperparameter mismatch risk. 10 → 290 is a 29× scale-up. Gradient-noise profile changes; the reference LR/EMA likely won't converge in 10 h without retuning, and we have no budget to retune.
  • Original-data dilution. With 290 train entries, each original entry is "seen" much less often per epoch. The augmentation should be a supplement, not the dominant signal.
  • Diminishing chemotype returns. ~246 of the unpicked are also XChem 5S* 2022 fragments (same screen as our 25). Adding hundreds of nearly-identical-screen entries doesn't broaden chemotype space meaningfully.
  • Wall-clock cost. Assembling 271 takes ~30 min + FT slows per epoch = less time for analysis and the deck.

If we get a v2 FT run (after seeing first-run results), scaling to 271 is on the table. But not before the first run — see Option B below.

Methodology appendix choices and why

ESM-2 model: esm2_t12_35M_UR50D

35M params is enough for the pocket-residue signal in a single well-studied protein. Faster on CPU than t33 (650M) and avoids the download. Downstream embeddings live in ℝ^480.

Pocket radius: 5 Å heavy-atom

Standard for binding-site definition. Yields 17–21 residues per complex — enough to embed, sparse enough to be chemotype-discriminative.

Morgan FP: r=2, 2048 bits

Industry-standard for chemotype similarity. r=2 is the sweet spot for fragment-sized molecules (r=3 over-fits, r=1 under-fits).

Butina cluster cutoff: 0.65

Initial 0.5 was too strict (20 micro-clusters across 27 ligands). 0.65 groups pairs with Tanimoto > 0.35, the chemotype-scaffold regime for fragments. Yields 15 clusters — interpretable.

Joint score: mean of protein + ligand similarity

Simple, interpretable. A multiplicative outer-product would over-penalise pairs where one side scores zero. Mean is a fair balance under our two-vote setup.

Pick policy: cluster coverage + diversity

3 picks per uncovered val ligand cluster (covers the generalisation probes) + 7 fillers from candidate clusters not yet sampled (broadens chemotype space). Total = 25.

MSA reuse: copy canonical, not generate

All 27 Apheris-supplied a3ms are bit-identical, and the 359 RCSB Q9Y233 entries share the same UniProt — so the canonical Apheris MSA applies to every PDE10A entry. We copy & rename. No alignment work needed.

Rebalanced split: 1 per cluster + 1 far

For the held-out eval, one entry per val ligand cluster (so we cover the chemotype space) plus 1 extra from the cluster furthest from train in joint similarity. The rest of the original val moves into train.
